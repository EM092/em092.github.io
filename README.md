<h3>Thomas Hope</h3>
<p>Thomas Hope was born into a wealthy family in Amsterdam in 1769. <br>
Thanks to  family’s banking firm, Hope was in a privileged position to be able to travel extensively during both his youth and later years, the most influential of which had been his eight year long Grand Tour. His primary destinations were in the Levant, Italy, and Greece. <br>
Hope moved to London in 1799, after Napoleonic France occupied Amsterdam.<br>
It was here that his skills in interior design could truly shine for the first time, for he made grand changes to the estate he moved into, drawing inspiration from Neoclassicism, an art movement which not only drew inspiration from, but actively seeked to recreate the aesthetics of Classical Greece and Rome.<br>
In 1807, Hope purchased another estate, this time, a 4hundred acre plot of land in Surrey called Deepdene. This house not only boasted Hope’s own designs, but also held a mosaic from Hadrian’s Villa - an artefact Hope had picked up during his Grand Tour. This practice had been common for people going on the Grand Tour - as people would take works of art and artefacts with themselves as souvenirs.<br>
Besides being a patron of the arts, Hope continued adding to his collection even after returning from the Grand Tour: he had more than 1500 antique vases, some of which he had purchased from Sir William Hamilton’s collection in 1801. Some of these are parts of the exhibition here, in the Classical Museum. 
</p>

<h3>How Did They End Up Here?</h3>
<p>Shortly after Thomas’ death in 1831, his son Henry sold parts of the vase collection and moved the remaining pieces to another family property; Deepdene in Surrey - as well as having the Duchess Street residence demolished. In 1917 Thomas’ grandson Francis sold much of the collection’s remnants, resulting in the distribution of the collection moving across Britain and America - landing in museums and private collections. <br>
The National Museum of Ireland and the Classical Museum of UCD later collaborated and jointly acquired about 20 vases. This effort was assisted by sponsorships from various prominent figures in Dublin. [sponsor info requested from NMI, waiting for response] <br>
The Classical Museum now displays a combination of our own vases from this collection, as well as some on loan from the National Museum of Ireland. 
</p>
